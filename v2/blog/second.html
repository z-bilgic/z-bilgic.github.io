Tanzania

June 2026 •

I'm not big on travel. I don't really ever remember enjoying a trip to a new place just for the sake of leaving. So when I agreed to go on a group trip to Tanzania, my mom was not the only one who was surprised--I was surprised too.

Still, I will forever be grateful that I was able to go, despite the 30+ hour trip and the fact that it prevented me from being able to attend programs and apply for several more. The gratitude on the faces of the families we passed out food to, combined with the beaming faces of children as we handed them lollipops will forever make the moment worth it.

I learned so much from my time in Tanzania, starting with the reminder of how sheltered I am in the United States. I was not even able to drink the tap water there, even though it is served for free in many restaurants here. My hearrt aches for the many children who I know go thirsty because their families are unable to afford bottled water, and the thousands more who are forced to resort to drinking water from rivers. Opening a well and seeing the village glow from how brightly its people were smiling stirred something in me. There is so much more I can do to help these people.

Another thing I learned to be more grateful towards is my ability to have an education. Not only is it difficult for boys to recieve an education past secondary school, but most girls stop after primary. There are many reasons for this, but one of them is that it is unsafe for them to walk to and from school. We live in the 21st century and we still cannot guarantee the safety of girls who are as young as 9 years old. It broke my heart.

Despite their hardships, however, the people living in Tanzania were some of the happiest people I have ever met. They were extremely welcoming and kind, always eager to share what little they had. Here in the US, we have so much more than them, and yet we are the greedily ungrateful ones. We can do so much more than that; we can be so much more than the obliviously ignorant citizens who complain selfishly.

Alas, their kindness and happiness in the face of hardship was inspiring, and it made me want to help them in every way I could. I will forever be grateful for the chance to go on this trip, and I hope others will also have the same opportunity to learn from the people of Tanzania and be inspired by their strength and kindness. After all, they have so much to teach us, starting with the phrase: Hakuna Matata.
